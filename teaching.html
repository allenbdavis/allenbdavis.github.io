Teaching

International School of Boston

I began teaching at ISB in August 2020, right after defending my Ph.D. Most of my time is spent in the upper school teaching physics, but I also teach mathematics, computer science, and — whenever the opportunity arises — astrophysics. ISB is a wonderful international community, and I feel lucky to work with students who bring so many different perspectives and curiosities into the classroom.

Physics

IB Physics is a two-year course fulfilling the science requirement for 11th and 12th grade students pursuing the International Baccalaureate Diploma. It covers a wide range of topics — mechanics, electricity and magnetism, waves, fields, and nuclear physics — with Higher Level students exploring additional territory including thermodynamics, quantum physics, and special relativity. I love this course because it consistently asks students to think like physicists, not just solve problems.

MYP Physics is a required one-year course for 10th graders. It introduces the core ideas of physics through hands-on investigation and real-world application, building the foundation students will need for IB and beyond.

I also advise the Astronomy Club, where students observe the night sky, discuss current events in space science, and occasionally tackle their own observational projects. It's one of my favorite parts of the school week.

  • 11th & 12th grade IB Physics, Standard and Higher Level (SY2020–present)
  • 10th grade MYP Physics (SY2020–present)

Mathematics

I've taught a range of math courses at ISB, from standard 10th grade math to advanced topics for motivated students in the French Baccalaureate track. I especially enjoy teaching math to students who don't yet see themselves as "math people" — there's something deeply satisfying about helping a student reach a moment of clarity on a concept they'd found intimidating.

Courses marked * are in the French Baccalaureate track, taught in English.

  • 10th grade Math Standard Level (SY2020–2021, SY2022–2023)
  • 11th grade Advanced Math Topics* (SY2023–present)
  • 10th grade Advanced Math Topics* (SY2022–present)
  • 9th grade Advanced Math Topics* (SY2022–present)
  • 7th grade Math Enrichment (SY2021–present)

Computer Science

Technology and computation are woven into modern science, and I think every student benefits from understanding how computers work and how to make them do useful things. I teach 10th grade Sciences Numériques et Technologie (SNT), a French Baccalaureate course that introduces students to digital technology — from networking and data to programming and cybersecurity. I also advise the Coding Club, where students get hands-on experience with Python and work on projects of their own design.

Courses marked * are in the French Baccalaureate track, taught in English.

  • 10th grade Sciences Numériques et Technologie* (SY2021–present)
  • Coding Club Advisor (SY2022–present)

Yale University

During my five years as a Teaching Fellow at Yale, I discovered something I hadn't fully expected: I loved teaching. Running weekly discussion sections gave me the chance to slow down and really engage with students' questions — to find out where understanding was breaking down and figure out, together, how to rebuild it. Working alongside Prof. Debra Fischer and Prof. Michael Faison, both gifted educators, taught me a great deal about how to make challenging ideas feel accessible and genuinely exciting. Those years in the classroom shaped the kind of teacher I wanted to become.

  • ASTR 130: Origins & Search for Life in the Universe, Prof. Debra Fischer (Fall 2015, Fall 2016, Spring 2020)
  • ASTR 135: Archaeoastronomy, Prof. Michael Faison (Spring 2016)
  • ASTR 120: Galaxies and the Universe, Prof. Michael Faison (Spring 2015)

Williams College

My journey as an educator really started at Williams, where I spent three years as a Teaching Assistant and Head Teaching Assistant at the Milham Planetarium and Hopkins Observatory. As Head TA at the planetarium, I presented shows to school groups, astronomy classes, and the general public — and I trained other student lecturers to do the same. There is something uniquely powerful about standing under a dome full of stars and watching someone experience the night sky in a new way. I also worked at the Hopkins Observatory, guiding students through telescope observations and one-on-one tutoring. More recently, I've returned to Williams as an invited guest lecturer in undergraduate astronomy courses covering planets, the Milky Way, and stellar physics.

Private Tutoring and Mentoring

Since 2019, I've worked privately with high school and college students across a range of subjects, including IB Physics, Chemistry, Algebra II, SAT Math prep, and Python programming. At the college level, I've tutored students in calculus-based electricity and magnetism — a course many students find to be a genuine turning point in their physics education.

Some of my most rewarding work has been mentoring students through independent research projects. Guiding a motivated teenager through their first real scientific investigation — helping them form a question, design a method, wrestle with unexpected results, and present their findings — is one of the most meaningful things I do. It's the kind of experience that can genuinely change a student's relationship to science.
