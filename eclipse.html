Eclipses

The first solar eclipse I remember seeing was in August of 2008, while I was traveling to Edinburgh to do theater tech for our school play at the Fringe Festival. Before we departed, I learned that we would be able to see a solar eclipse in Europe that we wouldn't have been able to see back home in the US — and I was genuinely thrilled. I bought some welder's glass and brought it with me on the trip. Ultimately, the partial eclipse occurred while we were on a train going from London to Edinburgh. I went to the very back of the train to gaze at the partially eclipsed sun through my welder's glass, and I must've shown a dozen other passengers before the moment passed.

A few years later I started at Williams College, where I got to know the renowned eclipse researcher Prof. Jay Pasachoff. Under his mentorship, I contributed to eclipse expeditions and co-authored scientific publications on the solar corona. My first total solar eclipse was in Gabon in 2013 — and I have been hooked ever since.

Eclipse photography is a discipline unto itself. The corona spans an enormous dynamic range — the inner corona near the limb is far brighter than the delicate streamers extending millions of kilometers into space — and totality lasts only a few minutes, leaving little room for error. Over the years I've developed techniques for capturing the corona across multiple exposures and processing them to reveal structure across this full brightness range.

I've been chasing eclipses since 2008. Below are all the annular and total solar eclipses I've witnessed in person. You can also view my full eclipse log — including partial eclipses — at Eclipse Chaser Log.

May 20, 2012 — New Mexico, USA (Annular)

My first annular eclipse, observed from the Karl G. Jansky Very Large Array in the high desert of New Mexico. The VLA is one of the world's premier radio observatories, consisting of 27 large dish antennas arranged in a Y-shaped configuration across the Plains of San Agustin. The site's remote location and high altitude make it an exceptional observing platform for radio astronomy — and, as it turned out, for eclipse watching. I witnessed 2 minutes and 21 seconds of annularity, the "ring of fire" effect that occurs when the Moon is near apogee and its apparent diameter is too small to fully cover the Sun.

November 3, 2013 — Lopé National Park, Gabon (Total)

My first total solar eclipse, observed as part of the Williams College Eclipse Expedition led by Prof. Jay Pasachoff. We set up in the village of Mkongo II in Lopé National Park, reached by a two-hour drive on unpaved roads. The eclipse path crossed only the Atlantic Ocean and equatorial Africa, so land-based observing options were limited. Meteorological forecasts gave Gabon roughly a 10% chance of clear skies. Sites further into Africa had better forecasts, but the totality duration there would have been too brief for our coronal imaging program. Gabon was selected despite the weather risk. Rain fell approximately twenty minutes before totality. Skies cleared in time, partly due to eclipse cooling, a documented effect in which the temperature decrease preceding the umbra can cause low clouds to dissipate. All of totality was observed under clear conditions.

Miloslav Druckmüller produced a high-resolution composite of the solar corona from over 100 of my photographs, together with images by Jay Pasachoff and Vojtech Rušin. A separate composite combining a ground-based coronal photograph from Gabon with ultraviolet data from the PROBA2 satellite and outer corona data from SOHO was featured as Astronomy Picture of the Day.

August 21, 2017 — Oregon, USA (Total)

The first total solar eclipse to cross the contiguous US coast to coast in nearly a century. Again with the Williams College team, I helped collect coronal imagery and scientific data, and witnessed 1 minute 55 seconds of totality. I appeared on WTNH's Good Morning CT to discuss the eclipse, and a photograph from the 2013 Gabon eclipse was featured on the cover of the American Astronomical Society's wall calendar that year.

April 8, 2024 — Texas, USA (Total)

My longest totality yet: 4 minutes and 14 seconds. I led a trip of approximately 70 ISB high school students to Texas to witness totality — the largest group I've ever brought to an eclipse.

In the days leading up to the eclipse, I worked with students to understand what they were about to see: why the corona becomes visible, what causes Baily's beads, how to safely observe the partial phases and when it's safe to look without filters. When the moment of totality arrived and the sky darkened, the students erupted. Some cried. Some just stood there silently. All of them were changed by it. That reaction — that visceral, unmediated awe — is exactly why I keep chasing eclipses, and why I think every young person should see a total solar eclipse at least once.
